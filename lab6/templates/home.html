History and Influences

Cuba is an island about 90 miles from the Miami coast. It was both the first and last Spanish colony in the area. What makes Cuban cuisine so special is that it is a direct result of Cuba's history. Of course, having been a Spanish colony, Cuban cuisine is heavily influenced by Spanish cuisine, specifically, the Andalucian region. It is a blend of Taino foods and Spanish, African, and Caribbean cuisines.

During the Colonial era, Havana was a very important trading port. The Spanish brough over cattle and pigs which was incorporated into the foods. When slavery was outlawed, large groups of Chinese indentured laborers were brough from China to do the labor. This had a noteworty impact on Cuban cusine. Perhaps most notably, the introduction of rice which is now a Cuban staple and served at almost every dinner.

After the Cuban revolution and after Cuba was cut off from its source of imports, they had to find other sources to keep the economy going. The island strengthened ties with the Soviet Union and new foods were introduced into the Cuban diet, including wheat, pasta, and yogurt. Where pork used to be the main food, thanks to the Spanish, now chicken became more prominent. Beef almost completely disappeared from the Cuban diet. In Miami, however, the Cuban food is heavily influenced by American cuisine.

Cuban Staples

Because of Cuba's tropical climate, tropical fruits and root vegetables are grown in abundance. These have heavily influenced Cuban cuisine. Starchy foods such as yuca, taro, and plantains play a central role in the Cuban diet. For instance, the dish known as tostones, consists of flattened, fried green plantains, an African dish that is a remnant of the influence the slave trade had on the island's cuisine.

Rice is another staple in Cuban cuisine. This is due to the Chinese influence on the Cuban diet. Many meals are always served with rice on the side. Dishes such as arroz con pollo, and moros y cristianos are dishes which feature rice as a main ingredient.

This next item is not a staple, but a method of cooking know as la caja china, or The Chinese Box. It is a method of cooking an entire pork, traditionally done at parties and holidays. Cubans learned this method of cooking from the Chinese laborers. It consists of a makeshift wooden box which places the fire on the top. This is an efficient method of cooking, and leaves the meat tender.
